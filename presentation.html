Context & Stakes

"Getting the wrong part shipped is just the beginning"

Industrial machines age 5–10+ years. Tribal knowledge fades. Docs accumulate. Three different stakeholders feel this pain in different ways.

🎯

Service Director

  • Wrong part = delayed repair chain
  • Costs: shipping, another tech visit, frustrated customer
  • Every wrong shipment is an avoidable failure
🔧

Field Service Tech

  • Combing 100-page PDFs, matching parts by sight
  • No visual context for the component in question
  • Customers send photos; internal team searches blueprints
🏭

End Customer

  • Unplanned downtime while waiting on the right part
  • Zero visibility into the identification process
  • Pays the cost of everyone else's friction
Worst case: wrong part shipped → delayed repair → shipping/parts costs → another tech visit → customer frustration. Even best case: delays and downtime.

Before

How parts are identified today

A multi-system manual process that degrades with machine age, new hires, and product variants.

  • Docs scattered across systems — no single source of truth
  • 100-page static PDFs with no spatial search
  • Each product variant means re-learning the catalog
  • Customers have no access — they send photos
  • New hires lack tribal knowledge built over years
  • Machine age compounds every one of these problems
[1:00]
Insight

The problem isn't parts — it's navigation

"If you can see it in 3D, you can find it."

The Insight

  • Tribal knowledge degrades as machines age 5–10+ years
  • New hires can't memorize 30,000+ part variants
  • Customers and operators change constantly
  • Too many product lines to train on every catalog

The Hypothesis

A 3D interactive digital twin turns a memory/search problem into a spatial navigation problem. If technicians can click what they see, the system returns the part number — no catalog expertise needed.

"I brought this idea to the VP of Service after seeing how 3D parts catalogs work in consumer electronics — a $50 microwave has better parts UX than a $5M industrial machine."

Technical Approach

Four inputs. One searchable 3D catalog.

The same data that exists in every industrial company — eBOM, CAD, assembly structure, ERP — becomes interactive when unified in the right platform.

Legacy feasibility challenges I mapped before building: Old CAD data required re-verification · Imperfect assembly structure needed manual cleanup · Scattered data sources required mapping effort before any build. "I mapped these gaps first so the PoC used real customer data, not synthetic demos."

Validation Plan

Accuracy first. Time second.

Formal testing is planned as the next phase. Here's the framework — and why the design matters.

In Progress — Testing Planned

Study Design

  • Within-subject comparison (same technician, both methods)
  • 6–8 field service technicians
  • 5 tasks with old method · 5 tasks with digital twin
  • ~60 data points total
  • Balanced task difficulty across participants

What We're Measuring

  • ✓ Accuracy first — did they identify the correct part?
  • ⏱ Time second — how long did each identification take?
  • Go / no-go threshold: meaningful accuracy lift + ≥30% time reduction
  • "This threshold isn't a claim — it's the bar we need to clear to recommend full rollout"

PoC demos have validated feasibility and usability. Formal measurement is next.

Why This Matters

The hard part isn't the technology

What this project actually demonstrates — and why it's directly relevant to selling industrial AI.

  • Integration is the moat. Getting CAD + PLM + ERP + BOM to talk to each other is where every industrial AI project lives or dies.
  • PoCs need a measurable threshold. Not "does it work?" — "does it clear the bar we set before we started?"
  • Stakeholders operate in parallel. Service Director, engineering, IT, and customers all have different definitions of success.
  • Working prototypes beat slide decks. A demo with real data moves faster than any ROI spreadsheet.
